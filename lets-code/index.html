Introduction

Before I let you all in on what I have in mind let me share a few thoughts that are at the core of my thinking regarding computer programming...

Most people have the ability to learn programming

So it is not a question of whether a person can learn how to program, it is a question of whether they'd like it enough to stick with it to the point that they can create software for themselves or develop a marketable skill.

Programming is much like learning the foreign language of a people with a totally foreign culture

I'm not much into Stark Trek but I suppose we can liken computers Vulcans in how they are governed by logic. It is your job to convey instructions to it in a logical way because computers will do exactly what you tell them to do; nothing more and nothing less. I guess they are also much like Noah in that they do "just so".

Natural languages are very imprecise

Unfortunately, English (as most if not all natural languages) is very imprecise. For example, take the statement "the man looked at the lady with the telescope". At first glance you might think you understand that statement. But question: who has the telescope? Take your time and think carefully. We'd love for you to post your answer in the WhatsApp group before selecting the answer below.



The reality is that the statement is too ambiguous to positively identify who has the telescope. The man could have been using the telescope to look at the lady or the man could have been looking at the lady who happened to have the telescope in her hand. Because natural languages are so imprecise we can't use them to communicate with computers. We need languages that don't leave any room for ambiguity in the instructions we are trying to give the computer. That is where computer languages come in. And there are hundreds of them; each with their pros and cons.
